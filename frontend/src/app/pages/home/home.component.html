Extinctions since 1500.

What does this mean?

From 1500 until well into the 20th century, virtually no extinctions were recorded among amphibians, mammals, birds, reptiles, and fish.

However, starting in 1900, the curve rose sharply and rapidly, reaching levels between 0.3% and 1.7% of the species assessed by the IUCN in 2018.

Although these percentages may seem low, they represent hundreds of species lost forever and reflect the direct impact of human activity—such as habitat destruction, hunting, and pollution—on our biodiversity.

This trend underscores the urgency of action today: every tenth of a percentage point hides an animal or plant that no longer exists, and protecting wildlife is everyone's responsibility.

Living Planet Index

Global trend in vertebrate populations since 1970.

What does this mean?

The Living Planet Index (LPI), developed by WWF and the Zoological Society of London, is a key tool for monitoring the health of global biodiversity. Based on more than 35,000 populations of 5,495 vertebrate species, the LPI indicates that, on average, these populations have declined by 73% between 1970 and 2020. This decline reflects the significant impact of human activities, such as habitat destruction, overexploitation, and climate change, on wildlife. The situation in Latin America and the Caribbean is particularly alarming, where populations have fallen by 95%.

Despite its usefulness, the LPI has received criticism for potential methodological biases. Some studies suggest that the index may overestimate population declines due to the disproportionate influence of certain populations in extreme decline. Furthermore, it has been pointed out that the LPI is based solely on monitored vertebrates, excluding many less-studied taxonomic groups, which could limit and undermine it's representativeness of global biodiversity.

Nevertheless, the LPI remains a valuable tool for visualizing overall trends in vertebrate populations and raising awareness about the importance of biodiversity conservation. Although it should be interpreted with caution and complemented by other indicators and studies, the LPI provides a crucial insight into the magnitude of biodiversity loss and underscores the urgency of taking action to protect our planet's wildlife.

The high number of endangered species within these groups underscoresthe urgent need to expand conservation efforts beyond iconic species, also addressing those that play fundamental roles inecosystems, such as pollination and nutrient recycling.

What are the consequences of this?

What does this mean for the enviroment and for us?

This extinction crisis represents one of the most serious threats to life on Earth . Although the number of extinct or endangered species may seem small compared to the planet's vast diversity, each species plays multiple essential roles in its ecosystem. These functions include crop pollination, pest control, water purification, and the formation of fertile soil. The loss of a single species can trigger ripple effects that affect many others, disrupting the natural balance and compromising the health of the ecosystem as a whole.

The disappearance of species is not only an ecological tragedy but also has direct consequences for humanity. Healthy ecosystems provide vital services such as food, medicine, and clean air. The loss of biodiversity can weaken these systems, increasing vulnerability to disease, reducing food security, and exacerbating the effects of climate change. Furthermore, the decline of pollinators, such as bees, threatens agricultural production and, consequently, the global economy.

It is essential to recognize that every species, no matter how small or seemingly insignificant, contributes to the complex fabric of life on our planet. Protecting biodiversity is not just a matter of conserving nature for its intrinsic beauty, but an urgent necessity to ensure a sustainable future for all life forms, including our own. Collective and conscious action is essential to halt and reverse this crisis before its effects become irreversible.

How did we get here?

What were some of the causes for things to get this bad?

The climate and biodiversity crisis we face today is not a natural accident, but the result of a production and consumption model that prioritizes accumulation and unlimited growth over ecological balance. This system drives a constant demand for new products, encouraging practices such as planned obsolescence, where devices are designed to fail or become obsolete quickly, forcing consumers to replace them frequently. This strategy not only generates mountains of electronic waste, but also depletes natural resources and increases greenhouse gas emissions.

The fast fashion industry exemplifies this problem. To meet the demand for cheap, short-lived garments, a huge amount of clothing is produced, most of which ends up in landfills or is incinerated shortly after purchase. This cycle of production and disposal contributes significantly to water and air pollution, and the emission of polluting gases. An alarming example is the textile dump in the Atacama Desert, Chile, where tons of discarded clothing from developed countries accumulate, severely impacting the local environment.

Furthermore, intensive food production, especially industrial livestock farming, and deforestation for raw materials such as timber and minerals have led to the massive destruction of natural habitats. These activities not only release large amounts of greenhouse gases but also threaten the survival of countless species and human communities that depend on these ecosystems. This development model, focused on short-term economic gain, has ignored the long-term consequences for the planet and its inhabitants.

What can we do about it?

So things are bad, what can we do about it?

The current environmental situation is deeply alarming. Scientific indicators show that we have passed several critical points, implying that future generations will inherit a planet radically different from the one we know. However, recognizing the severity of this crisis should not lead us to inaction, but rather to act with determination and hope.

At the individual level, there are actions that can make a positive contribution: reducing unnecessary consumption, choosing long-lasting products, recycling properly, saving energy and water, and supporting local conservation initiatives. These practices, while valuable, are insufficient on their own to reverse the magnitude of the environmental challenges we face.

It is essential to support and participate in broader collective efforts. Organizations such as the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), Greenpeace, and the Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) play crucial roles in protecting biodiversity and promoting sustainable policies. Furthermore, initiatives such as "This is My Earth" allow individuals to directly contribute to the conservation of critical habitats through transparent and participatory donations. UN-backed programs such as the UN Decade on Ecosystem Restoration seek to restore vast degraded areas, while movements such as "Right to Repair" promote the repair and reuse of products to reduce waste.

However, to effectively address the root causes of the environmental crisis, it is imperative to challenge and transform systems that prioritize short-term economic profit over long-term sustainability. The excessive exploitation of natural resources, driven by unsustainable production and consumption models, has led to ecosystem degradation and the depletion of biodiversity. Adopting an approach that values collective well-being, equity, and respect for ecological limits is essential to ensuring a livable future for all life forms on our planet.
